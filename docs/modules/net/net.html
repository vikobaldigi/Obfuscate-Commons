Metadata Awareness (NET)

Encryption hides what you say. Metadata reveals how, when, where, and with whom — and that's often more than enough.

What Is Metadata?

Metadata means “data about data” — but that phrase hides its real power. Metadata includes the time, location, device, and network used to send a message. It's not the content — it's everything around the content.

Your phone call might be encrypted, but metadata reveals who you called, how long you talked, and from where. No message needed. Just patterns.

Why Does Metadata Matter?

Metadata builds networks. It links people, places, and behaviors. Governments use it to track suspects. Tech companies use it to target ads. Anyone who controls metadata controls the map — not just of what you’ve done, but what you might do next.

As Jeffrey Pomerantz puts it, metadata is a system for managing complexity — but also for managing people.

Metadata as Infrastructure

Metadata powers everything: search engines, recommendation systems, government databases. It feeds into AI training, prediction engines, and surveillance platforms.

Lev Manovich calls it the “second layer” of digital culture — the invisible layer that tracks what we click, watch, share, and skip.

How Metadata Is Used

One leaked NSA document said it plainly: “We kill people based on metadata.” That’s how powerful it is. And it’s not just intelligence agencies.

Social media, phone companies, online stores — all of them collect and analyze metadata to shape what we see and how we behave.

What Can You Do?

You can’t fully erase metadata — but you can resist it. One strategy is obfuscation.

As Brunton and Nissenbaum argue, the goal isn’t invisibility. It’s confusion. Hide in the crowd. Spoil the data. Refuse to be sorted easily.
