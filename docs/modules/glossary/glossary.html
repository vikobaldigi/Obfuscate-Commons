Glossary of Terms

Key concepts introduced across the Obfuscate-OS platform.

📘 Technical Terms (A–Z)

AES (Advanced Encryption Standard)
Symmetric cipher widely used in digital security, known for speed and resilience.
Asymmetric Encryption
Uses a public/private keypair to allow secure communication without a pre-shared secret.
Block Cipher
Encrypts data in fixed-size blocks rather than one bit or byte at a time.
Blockchain
A tamper-resistant digital ledger built from cryptographically linked blocks.
Certificate Authority (CA)
An entity that issues digital certificates to confirm identities in public-key infrastructures.
ChaCha20
A stream cipher known for its speed and use in mobile-friendly encryption systems.
Cipher
An algorithm or technique that transforms plaintext into ciphertext — the core mechanism behind encryption.
CLI (Command-Line Interface)
Text-based environment for issuing commands directly to a system.
Code vs. Cipher
Codes substitute entire words or phrases; ciphers operate on letters or bits.
Consensus Mechanism
System for agreeing on blockchain data (e.g., Proof of Work or Proof of Stake).
Cryptanalysis
The study of breaking or weakening encryption systems.
Cryptographic Failure
When encryption fails due to poor implementation, not flaws in the math itself.
Cryptographic Hash
Function that maps input data to a fixed output — used for integrity checks and chaining blocks.
DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Organization)
A self-governing group coordinated via smart contracts on a blockchain.
DES (Data Encryption Standard)
An obsolete encryption algorithm vulnerable to brute force attacks.
Digital Identity
Online identity credentials secured with cryptographic keys or blockchain systems.
Digital Signature
A cryptographic marker verifying the authenticity of a message or transaction.
ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography)
A compact, efficient form of public-key encryption based on algebraic curves.
Encryption
Conversion of readable data (plaintext) into unreadable form (ciphertext).
Enigma
WWII-era rotor-based cipher machine used by Germany; famously broken by Allied cryptanalysis at Bletchley Park.
Frequency Analysis
A method to break ciphers by analyzing character occurrence patterns.
GPG
GNU Privacy Guard — open-source tool for encrypting, signing, and verifying messages.
Group (Algebra)
A set with an operation that combines elements, with identity and inverses.
Hash Function
Generates a short string (hash) from data; used in integrity checks and blockchains.
Keypair
One public key and one private key used in asymmetric encryption.
Man-in-the-Middle Attack
When an attacker intercepts communication between two parties without detection.
MD5
Deprecated hash function with known vulnerabilities; avoid in new projects.
Merkle Tree
A hash-based structure that efficiently verifies data consistency in blockchains.
Metadata
Descriptive information about data — like time, sender, and location.
Modular Arithmetic
Math where numbers loop around after reaching a limit (modulus). Used in cryptography.
Obfuscation
The deliberate introduction of ambiguity to resist analysis or surveillance.
One-Way Function
Easy to compute, hard to reverse — a core idea behind modern encryption.
POSIX
A set of standards that define Unix-like command-line behavior and file structure.
Prime Field
A finite field using a prime modulus. Preferred in cryptographic algorithms.
Proof of Stake
Consensus model where users stake tokens to validate blockchain transactions.
Proof of Work
Consensus model requiring computation to validate blockchain transactions.
Public-Key Cryptography
Encryption system using public/private keypairs to share secrets securely.
RSA
Early public-key encryption system based on prime factorization.
Smart Contract
Code that executes on a blockchain when conditions are met — used in DAOs and apps.
Stream Cipher
Encrypts data one unit at a time — ideal for live communications.
Symmetric Encryption
Uses a single key to both encrypt and decrypt data. Requires secure key sharing.
Terminal
A command-line interface to control and inspect systems without a GUI.
Transparency (Blockchain)
The state of blockchain data being visible and verifiable by all participants.
Vigenère Cipher
A substitution cipher that uses a repeating keyword to vary letter shifts.
Zero-Knowledge Proof
Cryptographic method that proves knowledge without revealing the actual information.

📙 Social + Political Concepts (A–Z)

Access
Refers to the usability and inclusiveness of encryption tools. Barriers to access create unequal digital protection.
Backdoor
A hidden method of bypassing encryption, often demanded by governments or embedded by corporations.
Decentralization
The redistribution of control away from central authorities toward users or networks.
Digital Resistance
Using encryption, obfuscation, and privacy tools to resist surveillance and corporate control.
Ethical Encryption
Encryption practices that prioritize users' rights, usability, transparency, and autonomy over corporate profit or government control.
Infrastructure
The underlying systems — technical, legal, and economic — that shape digital access and control.
Manovich Layer
Lev Manovich's idea of metadata as the “second layer” of digital experience — invisible, yet powerful.
Obfuscation vs. Secrecy
Secrecy hides; obfuscation confuses. Both resist surveillance, but with different strategies and power dynamics.
Privacy
The right to control who sees your data, when, and why. Includes both content and metadata.
Surveillance Capitalism
Business model where companies profit from monitoring, predicting, and influencing user behavior.
Trust
In cryptography, trust is distributed — ideally through math, not institutions. Who you trust determines your risk.
