Algebra of Cryptography (ALGEBRA)

Encryption works because certain math problems are hard to reverse. This module introduces the algebra that makes modern cryptography possible.

Why Algebra?

You don’t need to be a math expert to use encryption — but understanding its foundations helps you trust (and question) the tools. At the heart of encryption are a few key ideas from abstract algebra: groups, primes, modular arithmetic, and one-way functions.

Groups and Inverses

In algebra, a group is a set where you can combine elements and undo any operation. For example, with the numbers {0, 1, 2}, if adding two numbers always gives you another number in the set, and every number has an inverse, then it’s a group.

In encryption, we usually work in modular groups — where the numbers "wrap around" after hitting a limit (a modulus).

Modular Arithmetic

If a regular clock goes from 1 to 12, modular arithmetic is how you tell time. In mod 12, 9 + 5 = 2. The numbers loop back after reaching a certain value.

Modular arithmetic makes sure that encrypted data stays within a defined set of numbers. This is a key part of why cryptography can work securely in digital systems.

Prime Fields and Cyclic Groups

A prime field is a modular system where the modulus is a prime number. These are used because they don’t allow division errors or shortcuts.

Within these fields, we often use cyclic groups — groups generated by repeatedly applying one operation. This structure is predictable but hard to reverse, making it ideal for encryption.

One-Way Functions

A one-way function is something that’s easy to do, but very hard to undo without special knowledge.

  • It’s easy to multiply two prime numbers. It’s hard to factor their product.
  • It’s easy to exponentiate a number. It’s hard to find the original number from the result — this is called the discrete logarithm problem.

These are the mathematical traps that make encryption strong. Even if someone intercepts your message, they can’t “undo” the math without the key.

Elliptic Curve Cryptography

Modern encryption often uses elliptic curve cryptography (ECC). These are smooth curves plotted over prime fields, and they form a special kind of group.

ECC offers strong security with much smaller key sizes — which means faster encryption, less bandwidth, and lower energy use.
