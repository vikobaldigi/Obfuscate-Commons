Asymmetric Encryption (KEYPAIR)

Public and private keys reshaped how we build trust online — without needing to meet first, or ask permission.

Why Keypairs?

Symmetric encryption assumes a shared secret — sender and receiver both know the same key, and both must keep it safe. That works, but only if you’ve already established trust.

Asymmetric encryption breaks that model. You can share your public key openly, and anyone can use it to send you encrypted data — but only you can decrypt it with your private key. It’s a shift that makes secure communication possible without prior agreement or centralized control.

This isn’t just a cryptographic trick — it redefines who gets to initiate trust, and how digital identity is constructed.

How It Works

  • Public Key: Used to encrypt messages or verify signatures. Safe to distribute.
  • Private Key: Used to decrypt or sign. Must stay secret.

These two keys are mathematically linked — but computing the private key from the public key is infeasible if the math is sound. Common algorithms include RSA (factoring), ElGamal and DSA (discrete logs), and ECC (elliptic curves).

From Theory to Infrastructure

In 1976, Diffie and Hellman introduced the idea of public-key exchange. RSA followed shortly after, providing the first practical implementation.

Today, keypairs power much of the internet: HTTPS, SSH, email signatures, cryptocurrency wallets, and secure messaging. They make it possible to verify identity and confidentiality — even in open, adversarial environments.

The Trust Problem

If someone sends you a public key claiming to be from your bank, how do you know it’s legitimate? This is the classic “man-in-the-middle” attack — and it’s where public-key systems start to depend on external validation.

Certificate authorities (CAs) are one solution. They form the backbone of PKI (Public Key Infrastructure), but they also reintroduce centralized trust — and all the risk that comes with it.

Cautionary Notes

Keypairs are powerful, but not perfect. The math is strong, but real-world systems still fail:

  • Reusing keys across systems can leak context
  • Weak randomness undermines key generation
  • Padding and side-channel attacks target implementation gaps

In practice, security depends not just on theory — but on how code gets written, shared, and maintained.
