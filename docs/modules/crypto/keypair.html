Asymmetric Encryption (KEYPAIR)

Public and private keys — a foundational shift in trust, identity, and digital autonomy.

Why Keypairs?

In symmetric encryption, secrecy depends on a shared key — both sender and receiver must guard it. Asymmetric encryption turns this model inside out. Anyone can encrypt using your public key, but only you can decrypt it.

This isn’t just a technical shift — it’s a political and philosophical one. Public-key systems enable communication without centralized mediation.

How It Works

  • Public Key: Shared openly to encrypt messages or verify signatures
  • Private Key: Kept secret to decrypt or sign

The math ensures no practical path from public to private key. RSA, ElGamal, and ECC all rely on hard problems like factoring or discrete logarithms.

From Theory to Practice

Diffie and Hellman broke the assumption that secrecy required prior key exchange. RSA soon offered an implementation. Today, keypairs power HTTPS, SSH, digital signatures, and cryptocurrencies.

The Trust Problem

If you can’t verify whose public key you’re using, you risk interception. This is the “man-in-the-middle” problem.

Solutions like certificate authorities (PKI) introduce new centralization and require us to ask: who do we trust to validate identity?

Lessons and Warnings

Keypairs are powerful, but not foolproof. Key reuse, padding flaws, and poor randomness can still break the system. Cryptography is about implementation — not just equations.
