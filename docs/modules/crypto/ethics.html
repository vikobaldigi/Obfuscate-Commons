⚖️ Encryption Ethics (ETHICS)

Encryption doesn’t just protect — it reshapes power. Who it shields, who it excludes, and who it enables are deeply ethical questions.

Why Ethics?

Cryptographic systems aren’t neutral. As Phillip Rogaway argues, they rearrange power: who can speak freely, who can watch, and who cannot. Encryption resists surveillance — but also enforces exclusion. Its use is always political, whether acknowledged or not.

To ask how encryption works is also to ask who it works for — and who gets left behind when the tools are too opaque, too difficult, or too embedded in tech industry logic.

Protection vs. Obfuscation

Not everyone needs military-grade encryption. But everyone needs some way to shield themselves from extractive visibility. For some, that’s strong cryptography. For others, it’s blending in — obfuscation, noise, misdirection.

As Brunton and Nissenbaum remind us: “privacy” doesn’t always mean silence. Sometimes it means strategic confusion.

Accessibility and Autonomy

Many encryption tools are hard to use. Documentation is scarce. Interfaces are brittle. Even when people want to protect themselves, the barrier to entry is high.

That’s an ethical problem. Because if only the most technically fluent can encrypt — then protection becomes a form of privilege, not a right.

Making encryption accessible isn’t just UX work. It’s redistribution.

Law, Violence, and Regulation

Governments often frame encryption as a threat — a way for criminals to hide. But what gets missed in that framing are the everyday people who use encryption to survive: journalists, migrants, whistleblowers, organizers.

When laws restrict encryption, they don’t just target bad actors. They criminalize protection itself. And they put more trust in surveillance than in the people resisting it.

Toward an Ethical Practice

Using encryption is not a neutral act. Nor is teaching it. It’s a decision to prioritize safety over convenience, clarity over secrecy, and community over compliance.

We encrypt not to disappear — but to appear on our own terms.
