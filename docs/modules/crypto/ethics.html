⚖️ Encryption Ethics (ETHICS)

Encryption doesn't just protect — it reshapes power. Who it shields, who it excludes, and who it enables are deeply ethical questions.

Why Ethics?

Cryptographic systems aren’t neutral. As Phillip Rogaway argues, they rearrange power: who can speak freely, who can watch, and who cannot. Encryption resists surveillance — but it can also entrench secrecy for the powerful.

Encryption protects rights — but can also shield abuse. The tension between safety and freedom lies at the heart of every encryption debate.

Obfuscation as Resistance

Helen Nissenbaum and Finn Brunton frame obfuscation not as deception, but as refusal — a tactic within systems that demand legibility. Obfuscation disrupts surveillance by introducing noise, ambiguity, and unpredictability.

Unlike encryption, which must be precise and strong, obfuscation can be lightweight and everyday — randomized search histories, spoofed locations, artificial fingerprints.

The Dual Use Dilemma

Encryption is a dual-use technology: it protects dissent and journalism — and also organized crime. This complexity is often used to argue for backdoors or government keys.

But “lawful access” creates structural vulnerabilities. As history shows, these weaknesses often expand beyond their intended use.

Responsibility of Cryptographers

Rogaway writes that cryptographers are moral agents. What they choose to build — or not build — defines the limits of power. Cryptography encodes values.

  • Design systems that prioritize autonomy over compliance
  • Resist funding or partnerships that embed control or bias
  • Build with users, not just institutions, in mind

Questions to Ask

  • Who is allowed to encrypt — and who is excluded?
  • Who controls the keys?
  • What metadata remains exposed, and to whom?
  • Is trust centralized or distributed?
  • Who benefits most — and who is most at risk?
