⚖️ Encryption Ethics (ETHICS)

Who gets to hide, who gets to see — and what encryption makes possible.

Encryption Is Political

Encryption is not neutral. It redistributes power. It decides who can speak safely, who can be surveilled, who gets to disappear.

Governments may frame it as a threat. Activists may rely on it to survive. Corporations may sell it as a feature while hoarding your metadata.

Privacy vs. Secrecy

Privacy is not the same as secrecy. Privacy is a boundary. Secrecy is an intention. Encryption can support both — or be misused to undermine them.

Whistleblowers, journalists, survivors — they need encryption to protect dignity and truth. But so do abusers, fraudsters, and regimes. The same tools serve many ends.

Obfuscation as Protest

Not everyone can encrypt. But everyone can obfuscate. Add noise. Disrupt patterns. Confuse extractive systems.

As Brunton and Nissenbaum argue, obfuscation is not deception — it’s refusal. It’s a tactic of the weak against the strong. A politics of ambiguity.

Ethical Questions to Consider

  • Should encryption be a universal right?
  • What are the costs of criminalizing secrecy?
  • When does secure communication become a threat?
  • Who designs encryption standards — and for whom?

Ethics isn’t just about what encryption does. It’s about who it enables, who it excludes, and what values it encodes.
