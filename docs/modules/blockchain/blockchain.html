What Is a Blockchain?

A blockchain is a distributed ledger ‚Äî a linked list of data blocks, each cryptographically tied to the one before it. The chain is append-only: once data is recorded, altering it requires rewriting every block after it, plus convincing the network to accept the change.

Instead of relying on a central authority, blockchains use consensus mechanisms like Proof of Work (PoW) or Proof of Stake (PoS) to agree on state. The result is a kind of trust without a middleman ‚Äî but it‚Äôs not trustless. It just shifts where trust lives: into software, hardware, and incentives.

  • Public-Key Signatures: Transactions are signed by private keys, proving authorship and integrity.
